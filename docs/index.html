Fig. 1. Overview of the DPFT pipeline. The input x is decomposed into a low-frequency trend m and seasonal residual s via boundary-padded average pooling (kernel k = 25). The residual is projected into a d-dimensional token sequence and encoded by N = 2 Transformer encoder layers. The last-step trend value is re-injected as an additive skip connection before the heteroscedastic Gaussian output head, which maps the last-token representation to per-step (μ, log σ) ∈ ℝT. At inference, MC Dropout (S = 50 passes) decomposes total predictive variance into aleatoric and epistemic components.

Abstract

Deterministic neural forecasting models, including recent Transformer architectures, produce point estimates without predictive confidence, limiting their utility in risk-sensitive decision-making settings. To address this gap, we propose Decomposed Probabilistic Forecasting with Transformers (DPFT), an encoder-only Transformer that integrates a moving-average seasonal decomposition module with a heteroscedastic Gaussian output head, trained jointly under negative log-likelihood. We further incorporate Monte Carlo Dropout at inference to capture epistemic uncertainty and decompose total predictive variance into aleatoric and epistemic components. On the ETTh1 and ETTh2 benchmarks, DPFT achieves competitive MSE values of 0.0444 and 0.1143, respectively, outperforming the LSTM baseline by 5.1% and 12.3%. Code is available at anonymous.4open.science/w/DPFT-7A71.

Key Findings

01

Seasonal decomposition (Module B) is the primary driver of accuracy. Transformer+SeasonalDecomp alone achieves the best deterministic MSE on ETTh1 (0.0456), outperforming the standard Transformer (0.0469) without any probabilistic component.

02

The Gaussian head (Module A) adds uncertainty at negligible accuracy cost. Transformer+Gaussian Head matches the deterministic Transformer in MSE while providing calibrated σ estimates, consistent with NLL training balancing accuracy and spread.

03

DPFT achieves best MSE, MAE, and NLL on both datasets (ETTh1 NLL −0.1922, ETTh2 NLL 0.1322), confirming that decomposition and the probabilistic head yield consistent, complementary gains.

04

ETTh2 is substantially harder. LSTM MSE 0.1303 vs ETTh1 0.0467. Ours reduces ETTh2 error by 12% over LSTM, the largest relative gain across all comparisons.

Architecture

  • Input projection: Linear(1 → d_model=64) per time step
  • Positional encoding: Learnable nn.Embedding(L, d)
  • Encoder: N=2 × TransformerEncoderLayer (n_h=4, d_ff=256, p=0.4)
  • Gaussian head: Two independent linear layers → (μ, log σ) ∈ ℝᵀ; log σ clamped to [−6, 6]
  • Decomposition: m = AvgPool(Pad(x; k=25)), s = x − m; s → encoder, last-step m → additive skip before head
  • Loss: Mean Gaussian NLL over batch × horizon

Training Setup

  • Dataset split: 70 / 10 / 20 (train / val / test)
  • Normalisation: StandardScaler fit on train
  • Optimiser: Adam, lr=1e-3
  • Scheduler: ReduceLROnPlateau (patience=7, ×0.5)
  • Early stopping: patience=15 on val NLL (probabilistic) or val MSE (deterministic)
  • MC Dropout: S=50 stochastic passes; σ̄ = √(aleatoric + epistemic)
